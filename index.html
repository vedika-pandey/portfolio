ESG SaaS · Product logic

Translating ESRS requirements into product workflows for a CSRD compliance platform

Apiday, Paris
May – Aug 2023
The challenge

ESRS was designed as a reporting standard, not a product workflow. Translating it into software required interpreting what the regulation was asking for, how sustainability teams would gather the information internally, and how that logic could be structured into a usable reporting interface.

A large part of the work involved simplifying complex disclosure requirements into data collection flows, questionnaires, and reporting logic that companies could realistically navigate without needing deep regulatory expertise.

What the work involved
  • Structured ESRS disclosure requirements into client-facing CSRD questionnaires and reporting workflows
  • Translated regulatory data points into practical KPI architecture and data collection logic for software environments
  • Designed ESG data collection templates to support more consistent sustainability reporting across different reporting maturity levels
  • Drafted 10+ corporate ESG policies across environmental, procurement, and social themes to support broader sustainability governance needs
  • Worked across reporting logic, usability, and disclosure interpretation to bridge regulatory expectations with practical product experience
Observation
“The difficult part wasn’t interpreting ESRS itself — it was translating regulatory language into workflows that companies could realistically complete with the data, ownership structures, and reporting maturity they already had.”

Workshop design · Sustainability learning

Designing ESG decision-making simulations and sustainability workshops for corporate and academic audiences

Rebalance Institute · February 2026 – April 2026
The problem with sustainability learning

Most sustainability training explains frameworks and concepts in isolation. Much less attention is given to how sustainability decisions are made when organisations face operational constraints, budget limitations, conflicting priorities, or incomplete information.

This work focused on designing learning experiences that move beyond awareness — helping participants engage with sustainability as a decision-making and systems challenge rather than a purely reporting or communications exercise.

What I designed
  • Sustainability learning programmes for corporate and academic audiences translating ESG concepts into practical organisational decisions
  • Climate risk and trade-off simulations placing participants inside realistic resource, stakeholder, and sustainability dilemmas
  • Structured workshop content connecting business activities, operational decisions, and sustainability outcomes in concrete terms
  • Facilitation frameworks supporting stakeholder discussion, prioritisation, and collaborative decision-making around ESG and resilience topics
Design principle
“People rarely struggle to understand sustainability conceptually. The harder part is navigating trade-offs when sustainability goals compete with operational realities, incentives, timelines, and uncertainty.”

How I work

The same underlying logic across different types of engagement.

01

Diagnose

Understand the data landscape, ownership structure, and the gap between what’s required and what exists before designing anything.

Produces
Gap analysis · compliance mapping · data audit
02

Architect

Design the framework, data structure, methodology, and governance before collection begins. The architecture determines whether outputs hold up later.

Produces
KPI framework · GHG methodology · governance model
03

Build

Execute data collection, analysis, stakeholder coordination, and reporting under the actual conditions that exist — not the ideal ones frameworks assume.

Produces
Emissions model · supplier data · disclosure draft
04

Govern

Build the documentation and processes that make the work repeatable by internal teams. Ongoing dependency on external consultants is a failure mode.

Produces
Audit trail · process documentation · handover pack
Design for what’s real

Frameworks are built for ideal conditions. Organisations rarely operate in them. Good ESG work is designed around what exists — not what should exist.

Reporting isn’t the destination

A disclosure is an output. The more important question is whether the organisation has built processes it can repeat, improve, and own independently next year.

Complexity needs translation

Regulatory requirements, technical methodologies, and ESG risk need to be communicated differently depending on who’s making the decision. That translation is part of the work.
